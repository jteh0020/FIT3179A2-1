Exploring Malaysia’s Air Travel Landscape

This interactive dashboard explores Malaysia’s aviation ecosystem — from domestic routes that connect regions across the peninsula and Borneo, to international networks linking Malaysia with the world. The story concludes with a look at airport performance and airline fleet evolution, uncovering how the industry has adapted and recovered from the pandemic years.

1. Domestic Flight Insight

Malaysia’s domestic network forms the backbone of national mobility. The visualizations below show how airports such as Kuala Lumpur (KUL), Kota Kinabalu (BKI), and Penang (PEN) anchor the country’s air routes.

The map above highlights all domestic routes, while the bar chart below ranks the top 10 busiest routes based on passenger or flight volume.

2. International Flight Insight

Malaysia serves as a key aviation hub in Southeast Asia. The international route map visualizes flight paths that connect Kuala Lumpur and other airports to major cities around the world.

Below, the bar chart identifies the top 10 international routes, revealing Malaysia’s strongest air links to destinations such as Singapore, Bangkok, Jakarta, and Ho Chi Minh.

3.1 Malaysian Airport Performance

Airports are the pulse of Malaysia’s air connectivity. This visualization compares passenger movement across major airports from 2017 to 2024, showing how each facility contributes to the national aviation network.

Kuala Lumpur International Airport (KUL) remains dominant, followed by Kota Kinabalu, Penang, and Kuching. Smaller regional airports support crucial domestic links, ensuring balanced connectivity throughout the country.

3.2 Aircraft Movement Trends (2017–2024)

Aircraft movement data provides another lens into airport performance — reflecting both flight frequency and operational activity levels across Malaysia’s airports. This visualization highlights how KLIA, KLIA2, and Penang consistently led in movement volume, with evident dips during the pandemic and recovery in later years.

The chart captures annual takeoffs and landings for each airport, illustrating resilience and recovery trends across Malaysia’s aviation network.

4. Fleet Performance and Utilisation

The final section focuses on Malaysia’s airline fleet size, providing insight into capacity growth and industry recovery between 2017–2024. It reflects how carriers like AirAsia, Malaysia Airlines, and Batik Air adjusted operations to meet changing demand.

Airlines demonstrate steady recovery post-2021, with low-cost carriers expanding rapidly to meet renewed passenger demand.

4.1 Fleet Composition by Aircraft Type

This interactive pie chart shows Malaysia’s fleet composition by aircraft type across the years 2017–2025. Use the slider to explore how Narrow Body, Wide Body, and Regional Jet proportions evolved as airlines adapted their operations post-pandemic.

In recent years, Narrow Body aircraft dominate the Malaysian fleet, reflecting growing demand for short-to-medium haul regional routes, while Wide Body and Regional Jet numbers fluctuate in line with international and domestic service recovery.
